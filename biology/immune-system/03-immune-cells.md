# Immune Cells and Their Functions

## üåü The Simple Story (Ages 6-12)

### Meet Your Body's Superhero Team!

Your blood is like a river flowing through your body, and swimming in that river are billions of tiny superheroes - your immune cells! Let's meet them!

#### The Team Captain: White Blood Cells

All immune cells are called **white blood cells** (even though blood looks red!). You have different types, and each one has a special job:

### 1. The First Responders: Neutrophils üö®

**Who they are:**
- The most common white blood cell
- Like firefighters - first to arrive at danger!
- They live for only 5-6 days (like mayflies)

**What they do:**
- Rush to infections super fast
- Eat bad bacteria whole (like Pac-Man!)
- Can eat 20-30 bacteria before getting full
- When they die fighting, they become PUS (eww, but cool!)

**Think of them as:** Emergency responders who race to put out fires!

### 2. The Big Eaters: Macrophages üçî

**Who they are:**
- The name means "big eater" in Greek
- Live in your tissues, like security guards at a mall
- Can live for months or years
- Much bigger than neutrophils

**What they do:**
- Eat germs, dead cells, and garbage
- Send alarm signals to call for help
- Help heal wounds
- Can eat 100+ bacteria!

**Think of them as:** Garbage trucks that clean up and sound the alarm!

### 3. The Detectives: Dendritic Cells üîç

**Who they are:**
- Have long arms like tree branches ("dendrite" = tree-like)
- Live in tissues where germs might enter (skin, nose, gut)

**What they do:**
- Catch germs and study them
- Travel to lymph nodes (like police stations)
- Show other immune cells what the bad guy looks like
- Help train the special forces (T cells)

**Think of them as:** Detectives who show wanted posters to the police!

### 4. The Assassins: Natural Killer Cells üí•

**Who they are:**
- Called "NK cells" for short
- Born ready to kill (no training needed!)
- Patrol your body 24/7

**What they do:**
- Find cells infected with viruses
- Find cancer cells
- Kill them on sight!
- Don't need permission to attack

**Think of them as:** Ninjas who hunt down infected cells!

### 5. The Special Forces: T Cells üéñÔ∏è

**Who they are:**
- Trained in a special school (thymus)
- Very smart and specialized
- Come in different types (like different soldiers)

**What they do:**

**Helper T Cells (CD4+):**
- Like generals who give orders
- Tell other cells what to do
- Coordinate the whole immune attack

**Killer T Cells (CD8+):**
- Assassinate infected cells
- Very precise - kill only bad cells
- Use special weapons (proteins that make holes)

**Think of them as:** An elite army with generals and soldiers!

### 6. The Weapon Makers: B Cells üéØ

**Who they are:**
- Make special weapons called antibodies
- Can remember enemies for life!
- Each B cell makes antibodies for ONE specific germ

**What they do:**
- Recognize germs
- Make millions of antibodies (like sticky tags)
- Tag germs so other cells know to destroy them
- Remember germs so you don't get sick again

**Think of them as:** Factories that make custom weapons for each enemy!

### 7. The Memory Keepers: Memory Cells üì∏

**Who they are:**
- Special T cells and B cells that remember
- Can live for 50+ years (your whole life!)
- Stay quiet until the enemy returns

**What they do:**
- Store "photos" of old enemies
- If that enemy comes back, they wake up FAST
- Destroy the enemy before you feel sick
- This is why vaccines work!

**Think of them as:** The computer that stores photos of all the bad guys!

## üìö The Student's Guide (Ages 13-18)

### The Cellular Cast of the Immune System

All immune cells originate from **hematopoietic stem cells** in your bone marrow. These stem cells can become any type of blood cell. Let's explore each cell type in detail.

### Myeloid Lineage: The Innate Responders

These cells form the innate immune system - quick but non-specific.

#### 1. Neutrophils: The Abundant First Responders

**Basic Facts:**
- **Percentage:** 40-75% of white blood cells
- **Lifespan:** 5-6 days (shortest-lived)
- **Production:** 100 billion made per day!
- **Size:** 12-15 Œºm diameter
- **Appearance:** Multi-lobed nucleus (3-5 lobes), granular cytoplasm

**Functions:**

**Phagocytosis:**
1. Detect bacteria via receptors
2. Engulf bacteria into phagosome
3. Fuse with granules containing enzymes
4. Kill using:
   - Reactive oxygen species (bleach-like chemicals)
   - Antimicrobial peptides
   - Enzymes (lysozyme, myeloperoxidase)

**NETosis:**
- Neutrophils can explode and release DNA nets
- NETs (Neutrophil Extracellular Traps) catch bacteria
- Like spider webs trapping flies
- DNA + antimicrobial proteins = deadly trap

**Degranulation:**
- Release toxic granule contents
- Kills extracellular pathogens
- Also damages tissue (collateral damage)

**Clinical Significance:**
- **Low neutrophils (neutropenia):** Severe infections, often bacterial
- **High neutrophils (neutrophilia):** Active infection or inflammation
- Pus = dead neutrophils + dead bacteria

#### 2. Monocytes/Macrophages: The Versatile Phagocytes

**Monocytes (Blood):**
- **Percentage:** 2-10% of white blood cells
- **Lifespan:** 1-3 days in blood
- **Size:** 15-30 Œºm (largest white blood cell)
- **Appearance:** Kidney-shaped nucleus

**Macrophages (Tissues):**
- Monocytes differentiate into macrophages when they enter tissues
- **Lifespan:** Months to years
- **Names vary by tissue:**
  - Kupffer cells (liver)
  - Alveolar macrophages (lung)
  - Osteoclasts (bone)
  - Microglia (brain)
  - Histiocytes (connective tissue)

**Functions:**

**1. Phagocytosis:**
- More efficient than neutrophils
- Can digest larger particles
- Eat dead cells, debris, pathogens

**2. Antigen Presentation:**
- Process pathogens into peptides
- Display peptides on MHC-II molecules
- Activate T cells (bridge innate ‚Üí adaptive)

**3. Cytokine Secretion:**
- IL-1, IL-6, TNF-Œ± (pro-inflammatory)
- IL-10, TGF-Œ≤ (anti-inflammatory)
- Coordinate immune responses

**4. Tissue Homeostasis:**
- Clear dead cells without inflammation
- Tissue remodeling
- Wound healing

**Polarization States:**
- **M1 (Classical):** Pro-inflammatory, kill pathogens
  - Stimulated by: IFN-Œ≥, LPS
  - Produce: TNF-Œ±, NO, ROS
- **M2 (Alternative):** Anti-inflammatory, tissue repair
  - Stimulated by: IL-4, IL-13
  - Produce: IL-10, TGF-Œ≤, growth factors

#### 3. Dendritic Cells: The Professional Antigen Presenters

**Basic Facts:**
- **Percentage:** <1% of blood cells
- **Location:** Tissues (skin, mucosal surfaces) and lymphoid organs
- **Lifespan:** Days to weeks
- **Appearance:** Multiple long projections (dendrites)

**Types:**
- **Conventional DCs (cDCs):** Activate T cells
  - cDC1: Cross-present antigens, activate CD8+ T cells
  - cDC2: Present to CD4+ T cells
- **Plasmacytoid DCs (pDCs):** Produce massive amounts of type I interferons (antiviral)

**Functions:**

**Sentinel Function:**
- Station themselves at entry points (skin, gut, lung)
- Sample environment constantly
- Detect pathogens via pattern recognition receptors

**Antigen Capture:**
- Phagocytosis
- Macropinocytosis (drink large volumes of fluid)
- Receptor-mediated endocytosis

**Migration:**
- Immature DC in tissue ‚Üí encounters pathogen
- Maturation triggered ‚Üí loss of endocytic capacity
- Upregulate CCR7 receptor
- Migrate to draining lymph node (following CCL19/CCL21 chemokines)

**T Cell Activation:**
- Display antigens on MHC-I and MHC-II
- Express high levels of co-stimulatory molecules (CD80, CD86)
- Secrete cytokines that polarize T cell responses
- Each DC can activate multiple T cells

**Why They're Special:**
- Most potent activators of naive T cells
- Bridge innate and adaptive immunity
- Determine the type of adaptive response (Th1 vs. Th2 vs. Treg)

#### 4. Eosinophils: The Parasite Fighters

**Basic Facts:**
- **Percentage:** 1-6% of white blood cells
- **Lifespan:** 8-12 days
- **Size:** 12-17 Œºm
- **Appearance:** Bi-lobed nucleus, large orange/red granules (eosinophilic)

**Functions:**

**Anti-Parasitic:**
- Too large for phagocytosis ‚Üí degranulation
- Release toxic proteins:
  - Major Basic Protein (MBP)
  - Eosinophil Peroxidase (EPO)
  - Eosinophil Cationic Protein (ECP)
- Damage parasite membranes

**Allergic Responses:**
- Recruited by IL-5, eotaxin
- Contribute to allergic inflammation
- Tissue damage in asthma, allergic rhinitis

**Modulatory Roles:**
- Produce cytokines (IL-4, IL-13)
- Regulate Th2 responses
- Tissue remodeling

#### 5. Basophils and Mast Cells: The Allergy Mediators

**Basophils:**
- **Percentage:** <1% of white blood cells (rarest)
- **Lifespan:** 1-2 days
- **Appearance:** Dark purple granules, S-shaped nucleus

**Mast Cells:**
- **Location:** Reside in tissues (near blood vessels, nerves)
- **Lifespan:** Weeks to months
- **Appearance:** Large, abundant granules

**Functions:**

**Allergic Reactions:**
- Express high-affinity IgE receptors (FcŒµRI)
- IgE antibodies bind to receptors
- Allergen cross-links IgE ‚Üí activation
- Immediate degranulation (seconds to minutes)

**Degranulation:**
- Release pre-formed mediators:
  - **Histamine** - vasodilation, increased permeability
  - **Heparin** - anticoagulant
  - **Tryptase** - protease
- Synthesize new mediators:
  - **Prostaglandins** - inflammation, pain
  - **Leukotrienes** - bronchoconstriction, vascular permeability

**Anaphylaxis:**
- Systemic mast cell activation
- Massive histamine release
- Blood pressure drops, airways constrict
- Medical emergency (epinephrine needed)

**Beneficial Roles:**
- Anti-venom responses
- Anti-parasite immunity
- Wound healing

### Lymphoid Lineage: The Adaptive Specialists

#### 6. T Lymphocytes (T Cells): The Orchestrators and Killers

**Basic Facts:**
- **Percentage:** 20-40% of white blood cells
- **Lifespan:** Days (effector) to years/decades (memory)
- **Size:** 7-8 Œºm (small)
- **Appearance:** Large nucleus, thin cytoplasm rim

**Development:**
- Born in bone marrow
- Mature in **thymus** (T = thymus-derived)
- Selection process:
  - Positive selection: Recognize self-MHC ‚Üí survive
  - Negative selection: Strongly recognize self-antigens ‚Üí die
  - Only ~2% survive to become naive T cells

**T Cell Receptor (TCR):**
- Each T cell has unique TCR
- Randomly generated (V(D)J recombination)
- ~10‚Å∑-10‚Å∏ different TCRs possible
- Recognizes peptide-MHC complexes (NOT free antigen!)

**Major Subsets:**

**CD4+ Helper T Cells (Th):**
- ~60% of T cells
- Recognize peptides on MHC Class II
- "Helpers" - coordinate immune responses
- Don't kill directly

**Subsets based on function:**

**Th1 Cells:**
- **Induced by:** IL-12, IFN-Œ≥
- **Key transcription factor:** T-bet
- **Produce:** IFN-Œ≥, TNF-Œ±, IL-2
- **Function:** Activate macrophages, fight intracellular pathogens
- **Pathogens:** Viruses, intracellular bacteria (TB, Salmonella)
- **Diseases:** Autoimmunity (Type 1 diabetes, MS, IBD)

**Th2 Cells:**
- **Induced by:** IL-4
- **Key transcription factor:** GATA3
- **Produce:** IL-4, IL-5, IL-13
- **Function:** Help B cells, recruit eosinophils
- **Pathogens:** Parasitic worms (helminths)
- **Diseases:** Allergies, asthma

**Th17 Cells:**
- **Induced by:** TGF-Œ≤ + IL-6 (or IL-21)
- **Key transcription factor:** RORŒ≥t
- **Produce:** IL-17A, IL-17F, IL-22
- **Function:** Recruit neutrophils, barrier immunity
- **Pathogens:** Extracellular bacteria, fungi (Candida)
- **Diseases:** Autoimmunity (rheumatoid arthritis, psoriasis)

**Regulatory T Cells (Tregs):**
- **Markers:** CD4+, CD25high, FoxP3+
- **Induced by:** TGF-Œ≤ alone
- **Produce:** IL-10, TGF-Œ≤
- **Function:** Suppress immune responses
- **Importance:** Prevent autoimmunity, maintain tolerance
- **Deficiency:** IPEX syndrome (fatal autoimmunity)

**Follicular Helper T Cells (Tfh):**
- **Location:** Germinal centers in lymph nodes
- **Markers:** CXCR5+, PD-1+, ICOS+
- **Transcription factor:** Bcl-6
- **Produce:** IL-21
- **Function:** Help B cells in germinal centers
  - Induce class switching
  - Promote affinity maturation
  - Select high-affinity B cells

**CD8+ Cytotoxic T Cells (CTLs):**
- ~40% of T cells
- Recognize peptides on MHC Class I
- Kill infected or cancerous cells
- Essential for antiviral immunity

**Killing Mechanisms:**
1. **Perforin/Granzyme:**
   - Form pore in target membrane
   - Inject granzymes (proteases)
   - Target undergoes apoptosis
2. **Fas-FasL:**
   - CTL expresses FasL
   - Binds Fas on target
   - Triggers apoptosis pathway

**Specificity:**
- Only kill cells displaying specific antigen
- Spare healthy cells
- Can kill serial targets (10-20 cells)

#### 7. B Lymphocytes (B Cells): The Antibody Factories

**Basic Facts:**
- **Percentage:** 5-15% of white blood cells
- **Lifespan:** Days (naive) to years (memory)
- **Size:** 7-8 Œºm
- **Appearance:** Similar to T cells

**Development:**
- Develop in **bone marrow** (B = bone marrow-derived)
- Each B cell expresses unique B cell receptor (BCR)
- BCR = membrane-bound antibody
- Selection removes self-reactive B cells

**BCR Diversity:**
- V(D)J recombination (heavy chain)
- V(J) recombination (light chain)
- Combinatorial diversity: ~10¬π¬π unique BCRs
- Each B cell has ONE specificity

**Activation Process:**

**1. Antigen Recognition:**
- BCR binds free antigen (NOT on MHC!)
- Antigen internalized, processed
- Peptides displayed on MHC-II

**2. T Cell Help (for most antigens):**
- Helper T cell recognizes peptide-MHC-II
- CD40L-CD40 interaction
- Cytokines (IL-4, IL-21)

**3. Proliferation:**
- Clonal expansion (one cell ‚Üí thousands)
- Migration to germinal center

**4. Differentiation:**

**Plasma Cells:**
- Antibody-secreting factories
- Secrete 2,000-20,000 antibodies per second!
- Lifespan: 3-5 days (short-lived) or years (long-lived)
- Long-lived plasma cells migrate to bone marrow
- Provide continuous antibody protection

**Memory B Cells:**
- Long-lived (years to lifetime)
- Rapid response upon re-exposure
- Higher affinity antibodies (from somatic hypermutation)
- Can quickly become plasma cells

**Antibody Functions:**
1. **Neutralization:** Block pathogen binding to cells
2. **Opsonization:** Tag pathogens for phagocytosis
3. **Complement activation:** Trigger complement cascade
4. **ADCC:** Direct NK cells to kill target
5. **Agglutination:** Clump pathogens together

**Antibody Classes:**

| Class | Structure | Location | Function |
|-------|-----------|----------|----------|
| IgM | Pentamer | Blood | First responder, strong complement activation |
| IgG | Monomer | Blood, tissues | Most abundant, crosses placenta, long-lasting |
| IgA | Dimer | Mucosa, secretions | Mucosal immunity, blocks attachment |
| IgE | Monomer | Tissues | Allergies, anti-parasite |
| IgD | Monomer | B cell surface | B cell receptor |

#### 8. Natural Killer (NK) Cells: The Innate Killers

**Basic Facts:**
- **Percentage:** 5-20% of lymphocytes
- **Lifespan:** ~2 weeks
- **Size:** 12-15 Œºm (larger than T/B cells)
- **Appearance:** Large granular lymphocytes

**Unique Features:**
- Lymphoid origin BUT innate function
- No antigen-specific receptors (no TCR, BCR)
- Don't require prior sensitization
- Kill without "permission"

**Recognition Strategy:**

**Balance of Signals:**
NK cells have:
- **Inhibitory receptors:** Recognize MHC-I
- **Activating receptors:** Recognize stress ligands

**Normal cells:**
- Express MHC-I ‚Üí inhibitory signal
- No stress ligands ‚Üí no activating signal
- Result: No killing

**Infected/Cancer cells:**
- Often downregulate MHC-I (evade T cells)
- Express stress ligands (NKG2D ligands, MICA/MICB)
- Result: Activating signals > inhibitory ‚Üí NK cell kills

**"Missing Self" Hypothesis:**
- Normal cells = "self" signals (MHC-I) present
- Abnormal cells = "self" signals missing
- NK cells kill cells missing "self"

**Killing Mechanisms:**
1. **Perforin/Granzyme** (like CTLs)
2. **ADCC:** Recognize antibody-coated cells (FcŒ≥RIII/CD16)
3. **TRAIL/FasL:** Death receptor pathways

**Cytokine Production:**
- IFN-Œ≥ (activate macrophages, promote Th1)
- TNF-Œ± (inflammation)
- GM-CSF (stimulate myeloid cells)

**Clinical:**
- Critical for viral infections (especially herpesviruses)
- Cancer immunosurveillance
- NK deficiency ‚Üí severe herpesvirus infections

#### Antigen Presentation: The MHC System

**MHC (Major Histocompatibility Complex):**
- Also called HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen) in humans
- Most polymorphic genes in human genome
- Why? Pathogen diversity, need broad coverage

**MHC Class I (All nucleated cells):**
- **Structure:** Œ± chain (3 domains) + Œ≤2-microglobulin
- **Peptide binding groove:** Closed ends (8-10 aa peptides)
- **Genes:** HLA-A, HLA-B, HLA-C
- **Function:** Display endogenous antigens (intracellular proteins)
- **Recognized by:** CD8+ T cells

**MHC Class II (APCs only):**
- **Structure:** Œ± chain + Œ≤ chain (each 2 domains)
- **Peptide binding groove:** Open ends (13-25 aa peptides)
- **Genes:** HLA-DR, HLA-DQ, HLA-DP
- **Function:** Display exogenous antigens (phagocytosed)
- **Recognized by:** CD4+ T cells

**Polymorphism:**
- Each person inherits 3 MHC-I and 3 MHC-II from each parent
- Total: 6 MHC-I molecules, 6+ MHC-II molecules
- Codominant expression (both alleles expressed)
- Population-level diversity ensures some individuals resist new pathogens

**Clinical Relevance:**
- **Transplantation:** MHC matching crucial (prevent rejection)
- **Disease associations:**
  - HLA-B27: Ankylosing spondylitis
  - HLA-DR3/DR4: Type 1 diabetes
  - HLA-DQ2/DQ8: Celiac disease

**Cross-Presentation:**
- Dendritic cells can present exogenous antigens on MHC-I
- Normally: MHC-I = endogenous, MHC-II = exogenous
- Cross-presentation allows CD8+ T cell activation against viruses that don't infect DCs
- Critical for antiviral and anti-tumor immunity

#### NK Cell Education and Licensing

**Problem:**
- NK cells kill cells lacking MHC-I
- But NK cells encounter low MHC-I cells during development
- How do they learn not to kill everything?

**Education Process:**

**1. Licensing (Arming):**
- During development in bone marrow
- NK cells that engage MHC-I via inhibitory receptors become "licensed"
- Licensed NK cells are functionally competent
- Unlicensed NK cells are hyporesponsive

**Mechanism:**
```
Inhibitory receptor (KIR, NKG2A) binds self-MHC-I
      ‚Üì
Continuous tonic inhibition during development
      ‚Üì
Paradoxically makes NK cell MORE responsive when inhibition is released
      ‚Üì
Result: Licensed, functional NK cell
```

**2. Disarming:**
- NK cells that never engage MHC-I become "disarmed"
- Low killing capacity
- Prevents autoreactivity

**"Missing-Self" Refined:**
- Not just absence of MHC-I
- But absence of the SPECIFIC MHC-I that licensed the NK cell
- Self-MHC-I missing ‚Üí activating signals dominate ‚Üí kill

**Receptor Diversity:**

**Inhibitory Receptors (recognize MHC-I):**

**Humans:**
- **KIRs (Killer Immunoglobulin Receptors):** ~15 genes, highly polymorphic
  - KIR2DL: Bind HLA-C
  - KIR3DL: Bind HLA-A, HLA-B
- **NKG2A/CD94:** Bind HLA-E (non-classical MHC-I)

**Mice:**
- **Ly49 receptors:** C-type lectins, ~20 genes

**Activating Receptors (recognize stress ligands):**
- **NKG2D:** Binds MICA, MICB, ULBP (stress-induced)
- **NCRs (Natural Cytotoxicity Receptors):** NKp30, NKp44, NKp46
- **2B4, DNAM-1:** Adhesion/activation

**Integration:**
```
Œ£(Activating signals) vs. Œ£(Inhibitory signals)
      ‚Üì
Activating > Inhibitory ‚Üí KILL
Inhibitory > Activating ‚Üí SPARE
```

## Clinical Correlations

### Primary Immunodeficiencies

**B Cell Defects:**
- **X-linked agammaglobulinemia (XLA):** BTK mutation, no B cells
  - Recurrent bacterial infections (Streptococcus, Haemophilus)
  - Treatment: IVIG (antibody replacement)

**T Cell Defects:**
- **SCID (Severe Combined Immunodeficiency):** Multiple genetic causes
  - No T cells (and often B cells)
  - Fatal without bone marrow transplant
  - "Bubble boy disease"

**Phagocyte Defects:**
- **Chronic Granulomatous Disease (CGD):** NADPH oxidase defect
  - No respiratory burst, can't kill bacteria
  - Catalase-positive bacteria survive (Staph aureus, Aspergillus)
  - Granulomas form (unsuccessful clearance)

**Complement Defects:**
- **C3 deficiency:** Recurrent bacterial infections
- **MAC deficiencies (C5-C9):** Neisseria infections (meningitis, gonorrhea)

### Therapeutic Manipulation of Immune Cells

**CAR-T Cells (Chimeric Antigen Receptor T cells):**
```

      ‚Üì
```

**Successes:**
- B cell malignancies (ALL, DLBCL) targeting CD19

- Neurotoxicity

- Anti-PD-1, Anti-PD-L1: Release T cell "brakes"
- Anti-CTLA-4: Enhance T cell activation
- Revolutionized cancer treatment (melanoma, lung cancer)
