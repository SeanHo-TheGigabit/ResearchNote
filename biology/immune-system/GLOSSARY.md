## ðŸ“š Basic Terms (Beginner Level)

### A

**Adaptive Immunity**
The "slow but smart" part of your immune system that learns and remembers specific germs. Takes days to activate but provides long-lasting protection. Includes T cells and B cells.

**Antibody**
A Y-shaped protein that sticks to germs like a name tag, marking them for destruction. Made by B cells. Each antibody recognizes one specific germ.

**Antigen**
Any substance (usually part of a germ) that your immune system can recognize as foreign. The "target" that antibodies and T cells look for.

### B

**Bacteria**
Single-celled germs that can cause infections. Some are helpful (in your gut), others are harmful (cause disease).

**B Cell**
A type of white blood cell that makes antibodies. Acts like a weapons factory, producing millions of antibodies to tag specific germs.

**Barrier**
Physical walls that keep germs out, like your skin, mucus in your nose, and stomach acid.

### C

**Cell**
The tiny building blocks that make up your body. Immune cells are specialized cells that fight germs.

**Cytokine** (sigh-toh-kine)
Chemical messenger molecules that immune cells use to "talk" to each other. Like text messages between cells.

### G

**Germ**
A tiny organism (bacteria, virus, fungus, or parasite) that can make you sick. Your immune system fights germs.

### I

**Immune System**
Your body's defense team that protects you from germs and disease. Made up of cells, tissues, and organs working together.

**Infection**
When germs invade your body and multiply, potentially making you sick.

**Inflammation** (in-fluh-MAY-shun)
Your body's alarm response when injured or infected. Causes redness, heat, swelling, and pain. Brings immune cells to fight infection.

**Innate Immunity**
The "fast but simple" part of your immune system you're born with. Responds immediately but doesn't remember germs. Includes physical barriers and basic immune cells.

### L

**Lymph Node**
Small bean-shaped organs throughout your body where immune cells meet and coordinate attacks. Swell when you're sick because cells are gathering there.

**Lymphocyte** (LIM-foh-site)
A type of white blood cell. Includes T cells and B cells - the specialized fighters of the adaptive immune system.

### M

**Macrophage** (MACK-roh-fayj)
"Big eater" cell that gobbles up germs, dead cells, and debris. Also sounds alarms to recruit other immune cells.

**Memory Cell**
Special T cells or B cells that remember germs you've fought before. Allow your body to respond faster if that germ returns. Why you rarely get the same cold twice.

### N

**Neutrophil** (NOO-troh-fil)
The most common white blood cell. First responders that rush to infections and eat bacteria. Make up most of pus.

### P

**Pathogen** (PATH-oh-jen)
A disease-causing germ. Includes harmful bacteria, viruses, fungi, and parasites.

**Phagocytosis** (fag-oh-sigh-TOH-sis)
"Cell eating" - when immune cells engulf and digest germs. Like Pac-Man eating dots.

**Pus**
Thick yellowish fluid made of dead neutrophils, dead bacteria, and tissue debris. Sign that your immune system fought an infection.

### T

**T Cell**
A type of lymphocyte (white blood cell) that either kills infected cells (killer T cells) or coordinates immune responses (helper T cells). Gets trained in the thymus.

### V

**Vaccine**
A treatment that teaches your immune system to recognize a germ without making you sick. Creates memory cells so you're protected if you encounter the real germ later.

**Virus**
A tiny infectious agent that hijacks your cells to make copies of itself. Smaller than bacteria. Causes diseases like colds, flu, and COVID-19.

### W

**White Blood Cell**
Immune cells that flow in your blood and fight infections. Many types including neutrophils, macrophages, T cells, and B cells. (Your blood has red blood cells too, which carry oxygen.)

## ðŸŽ“ Intermediate Terms (Student Level)

### A

**ADCC (Antibody-Dependent Cell-mediated Cytotoxicity)**
When antibodies coat a target cell, and NK cells recognize those antibodies and kill the target. Combines antibody recognition with cellular killing.

**Affinity Maturation**
Process where B cells improve their antibodies to bind germs more tightly. Happens in germinal centers through somatic hypermutation.

**Agglutination**
When antibodies cross-link pathogens together into clumps, making them easier to clear and preventing spread.

**Anergy**
A state where immune cells become unresponsive to their target. A form of tolerance to prevent autoimmunity.

**Antigen-Presenting Cell (APC)**
Cells that display antigens to T cells, activating them. Includes dendritic cells, macrophages, and B cells.

**Apoptosis** (ay-pop-TOH-sis)
Programmed cell death - cells dying in a controlled, non-inflammatory way. Used to kill infected cells and remove unwanted immune cells.

### B

**Basophil** (BAY-soh-fil)
Rarest white blood cell, involved in allergic reactions. Releases histamine when activated.

**Bone Marrow**
Soft tissue inside bones where blood cells (including immune cells) are made. B cells mature here.

### C

**CD4+ T Cell**
Helper T cells that coordinate immune responses. Recognize antigens on MHC Class II. Target of HIV.

**CD8+ T Cell**
Cytotoxic (killer) T cells that destroy infected or cancerous cells. Recognize antigens on MHC Class I.

**Chemokine**
A type of cytokine that attracts immune cells to specific locations. Creates chemical gradients cells follow.

**Clonal Expansion**
When one antigen-specific lymphocyte rapidly divides to produce thousands of identical copies after encountering its antigen.

**Complement System**
A cascade of ~30 proteins that punch holes in bacteria, tag pathogens for phagocytosis, and promote inflammation.

**Cytotoxic**
Capable of killing cells. Cytotoxic T cells and NK cells kill infected or cancerous cells.

### D

**Dendritic Cell**
Professional antigen-presenting cell with tree-like branches. Captures antigens in tissues, then travels to lymph nodes to activate T cells.

### E

**Eosinophil** (ee-oh-SIN-oh-fil)
White blood cell specialized for fighting parasites and involved in allergic reactions. Contains orange granules.

**Epitope**
The specific part of an antigen that an antibody or T cell receptor recognizes. One antigen can have multiple epitopes.

### F

**Fc Region**
The "tail" of an antibody that doesn't bind antigen but interacts with immune cells and complement proteins to trigger effector functions.

### G

**Germinal Center**
Structure in lymph nodes/spleen where B cells undergo rapid division, somatic hypermutation, and class switching after encountering antigen.

**Granulocyte**
White blood cells with visible granules: neutrophils, eosinophils, basophils.

**Granzyme**
Protease enzyme released by cytotoxic T cells and NK cells that enters target cells and triggers apoptosis.

### H

**Hematopoiesis** (hee-mat-oh-poy-EE-sis)
The production of blood cells (including immune cells) from hematopoietic stem cells in bone marrow.

**Histamine**
Chemical released by mast cells and basophils that causes blood vessel dilation, increased permeability, and inflammation. Responsible for allergy symptoms.

**Humoral Immunity**
Immunity mediated by antibodies in body fluids (blood, lymph). "Humoral" = relating to body fluids.

### I

**Immunoglobulin (Ig)**
Another name for antibody. Five classes: IgM, IgG, IgA, IgE, IgD.

**Immunological Memory**
The ability of the adaptive immune system to remember previously encountered antigens and respond faster upon re-exposure.

**Interferon** (in-ter-FEER-on)
Cytokines that induce an "antiviral state" in cells, making them resistant to viral infection. Named because they "interfere" with viral replication.

### L

**Leukocyte** (LOO-koh-site)
White blood cell - any immune cell in the blood.

**Lymph**
Clear fluid that circulates through lymphatic vessels, carrying immune cells and antigens to lymph nodes.

**Lymphatic System**
Network of vessels and organs (lymph nodes, spleen, thymus) that circulates lymph and houses immune cells.

### M

**Mast Cell**
Tissue-resident cell that releases histamine and other mediators during allergic reactions. Similar to basophils but live in tissues.

**MHC (Major Histocompatibility Complex)**
Proteins on cell surfaces that display antigens to T cells. MHC Class I on all cells, MHC Class II on antigen-presenting cells.

**Monocyte**
Large white blood cell in blood that differentiates into macrophages or dendritic cells when entering tissues.

### N

**Natural Killer (NK) Cell**
Lymphocyte that kills virus-infected cells and cancer cells without prior sensitization. Part of innate immunity.

**Negative Selection**
Process in thymus/bone marrow that eliminates immune cells strongly reactive to self-antigens, preventing autoimmunity.

**Neutralization**
When antibodies bind to viruses or toxins and prevent them from infecting cells or causing damage.

### O

**Opsonization** (op-son-ih-ZAY-shun)
Coating pathogens with antibodies or complement proteins to enhance phagocytosis. Makes pathogens "tastier" to phagocytes.

### P

**Pathogen-Associated Molecular Pattern (PAMP)**
Molecular structures shared by pathogens (like bacterial cell wall components) that are recognized by pattern recognition receptors.

**Perforin**
Protein released by cytotoxic T cells and NK cells that forms pores in target cell membranes, allowing granzymes to enter.

**Phagocyte**
Any cell capable of phagocytosis. Includes neutrophils, macrophages, and dendritic cells.

**Plasma Cell**
Differentiated B cell dedicated to antibody production. Can secrete thousands of antibodies per second.

**Positive Selection**
Process in thymus that ensures T cells can recognize self-MHC, allowing only functional T cells to survive.

### R

**Receptor**
Protein on cell surface that binds specific molecules. Immune cells have receptors for antigens, cytokines, etc.

**Regulatory T Cell (Treg)**
CD4+ T cells that suppress immune responses to prevent autoimmunity and maintain tolerance.

### S

**Self-Tolerance**
The immune system's ability to recognize and not attack the body's own cells and proteins.

**Somatic Hypermutation**
Process in germinal centers where B cell antibody genes undergo rapid mutations to improve antigen binding (affinity maturation).

**Spleen**
Organ that filters blood, removes old red blood cells, and houses immune cells that respond to blood-borne pathogens.

### T

**T Cell Receptor (TCR)**
Protein on T cells that recognizes specific antigen-MHC complexes. Each T cell has a unique TCR.

**Thymus**
Organ where T cells mature and undergo selection. Located behind the breastbone.

**Toll-like Receptor (TLR)**
Pattern recognition receptor that recognizes PAMPs and triggers innate immune responses.

### V

**V(D)J Recombination**
Genetic rearrangement process that generates diverse antigen receptors (TCR and BCR) by randomly combining V, D, and J gene segments.

## ðŸ”¬ Advanced Terms (University Level)

### A

**Activation-Induced Cytidine Deaminase (AID)**
Enzyme that initiates somatic hypermutation and class switch recombination in B cells by deaminating cytosines in immunoglobulin genes.

**Acute Phase Response**
Systemic response to infection/inflammation characterized by fever, production of acute phase proteins (CRP, serum amyloid A), and metabolic changes.

**Affinity**
The strength of binding between a single antigen-binding site and its epitope. Measured as dissociation constant (Kd).

**Allelic Exclusion**
Mechanism ensuring each lymphocyte expresses only one functional antigen receptor (one TCR or BCR) despite having two gene alleles.

**Anaphylatoxins**
Complement fragments (C3a, C5a) that trigger mast cell degranulation and inflammation.

**Anaphylaxis**
Severe, systemic allergic reaction caused by massive mast cell degranulation. Can be life-threatening.

**Autoimmunity**
Immune response against self-antigens, leading to tissue damage. Examples: Type 1 diabetes, rheumatoid arthritis, lupus.

### B

**B Cell Receptor (BCR)**
Antigen receptor on B cells, consisting of membrane-bound immunoglobulin plus IgÎ±/IgÎ² signaling molecules.

**Î²2-microglobulin**
Small protein that associates with MHC Class I heavy chain to form the complete MHC-I molecule.

### C

**C1q**
Complement protein that initiates the classical pathway by binding to antibody Fc regions.

**Caspase**
Protease enzyme family that executes apoptosis when activated. Cleaves cellular substrates leading to cell death.

**CD (Cluster of Differentiation)**
Standardized naming system for cell surface markers. Used to identify and classify immune cells (e.g., CD4, CD8, CD19).

**Class Switch Recombination (CSR)**
Process where B cells change their antibody class (e.g., IgM to IgG) while maintaining antigen specificity.

**Co-stimulation**
Additional signals (beyond antigen recognition) required for full lymphocyte activation. CD28-B7 interaction is classic example.

**Complement Receptor**
Receptors on phagocytes and other cells that recognize complement-coated pathogens, enhancing phagocytosis.

**Cross-Presentation**
Process where dendritic cells present exogenous antigens on MHC Class I (in addition to normal MHC Class II), allowing CD8+ T cell activation.

**CTL (Cytotoxic T Lymphocyte)**
Another term for CD8+ killer T cell.

**CTLA-4**
Inhibitory receptor on T cells that outcompetes CD28 for B7 binding, suppressing T cell activation. Target of cancer immunotherapy.

### D

**Death Receptor**
Cell surface receptors (Fas, TNFR) that trigger apoptosis when bound by their ligands (FasL, TNF).

**Degranulation**
Release of granule contents (histamine, proteases, etc.) from mast cells, basophils, or cytotoxic lymphocytes.

### E

**Effector Cell**
Activated immune cell performing its function (e.g., plasma cell secreting antibodies, CTL killing targets).

**Epithelial Barrier**
Physical barrier formed by tightly connected epithelial cells lining body surfaces (skin, gut, airways).

### F

**FasL (Fas Ligand)**
Protein expressed on cytotoxic T cells that binds Fas receptor on target cells, triggering apoptosis.

**FcR (Fc Receptor)**
Receptors on immune cells that bind the Fc region of antibodies, triggering various effector functions.

**Follicular Dendritic Cell (FDC)**
Specialized dendritic cells in germinal centers that display antigens to B cells for selection. Different from conventional dendritic cells.

### G

**GALT (Gut-Associated Lymphoid Tissue)**
Immune tissue in the gut including Peyer's patches, isolated lymphoid follicles, and lamina propria lymphocytes.

**Germline Configuration**
Unrearranged state of antigen receptor genes before V(D)J recombination.

### H

**Haptoglobin**
Acute phase protein that binds free hemoglobin, preventing oxidative damage during hemolysis.

**HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen)**
Human version of MHC. Highly polymorphic genes determining transplant compatibility and disease susceptibility.

### I

**Ig Superfamily**
Large family of proteins with immunoglobulin-like domains, including antibodies, TCRs, MHC molecules, and many co-receptors.

**Immunodeficiency**
Defective immune system unable to fight infections effectively. Can be primary (genetic) or secondary (acquired, like AIDS).

**Immunological Synapse**
Structured interface between T cell and antigen-presenting cell where signaling occurs. Organized into central and peripheral supramolecular activation clusters (cSMAC, pSMAC).

**Inflammasome**
Multi-protein complex that activates caspase-1, leading to IL-1Î² and IL-18 secretion and pyroptosis.

**Interferon Regulatory Factor (IRF)**
Transcription factors that induce interferon and interferon-stimulated gene expression.

**ITAM (Immunoreceptor Tyrosine-based Activation Motif)**
Signaling motif in receptors that, when phosphorylated, recruits kinases to activate the cell.

**ITIM (Immunoreceptor Tyrosine-based Inhibition Motif)**
Signaling motif that recruits phosphatases to inhibit cell activation.

### J

**Junctional Diversity**
Additional diversity in antigen receptors generated by imprecise joining and nucleotide addition at V(D)J junctions.

### L

**Langerhans Cell**
Dendritic cells resident in the epidermis of skin.

**Lymphoid Progenitor**
Bone marrow stem cell committed to lymphoid lineage (T, B, NK cells).

### M

**MAC (Membrane Attack Complex)**
Terminal complement components (C5b-9) that form pores in pathogen membranes, causing lysis.

**MALT (Mucosa-Associated Lymphoid Tissue)**
Immune tissue at mucosal surfaces (gut, respiratory, urogenital tracts).

**Marginal Zone**
Region of spleen containing specialized B cells that respond rapidly to blood-borne pathogens.

**Memory B Cell**
Long-lived B cell that can rapidly differentiate into plasma cells upon antigen re-encounter.

**MHC Class I**
MHC molecules on all nucleated cells that present endogenous antigens to CD8+ T cells.

**MHC Class II**
MHC molecules on antigen-presenting cells that present exogenous antigens to CD4+ T cells.

**MHC Restriction**
The requirement that T cells recognize antigen only when presented by self-MHC molecules.

**Myeloid Progenitor**
Bone marrow stem cell committed to myeloid lineage (neutrophils, monocytes, etc.).

### N

**NFAT (Nuclear Factor of Activated T cells)**
Transcription factor activated by calcium signaling in lymphocytes.

**NF-ÎºB (Nuclear Factor kappa B)**
Transcription factor that induces pro-inflammatory cytokine expression. Central to innate immune signaling.

**N-nucleotides**
Random nucleotides added by TdT at V(D)J junctions, increasing receptor diversity.

### O

**Opsonin**
Molecule that coats pathogens to enhance phagocytosis (antibodies, complement C3b).

### P

**P-nucleotides**
Nucleotides added at V(D)J junctions due to hairpin opening, contributing to diversity.

**Paracrine**
Signaling to nearby cells (as opposed to autocrine = self-signaling, or endocrine = distant signaling via bloodstream).

**Peyer's Patch**
Organized lymphoid follicle in the small intestine that samples gut antigens.

**Primary Immune Response**
First immune response to a novel antigen. Slower and weaker than secondary response.

**PRR (Pattern Recognition Receptor)**
Receptors that recognize conserved pathogen structures (PAMPs). Includes TLRs, NLRs, RLRs.

### R

**RAG (Recombination Activating Genes)**
RAG1 and RAG2 enzymes that catalyze V(D)J recombination.

**Reactive Oxygen Species (ROS)**
Toxic oxygen radicals (superoxide, hydrogen peroxide, hypochlorous acid) produced by phagocytes to kill pathogens.

**Respiratory Burst**
Rapid production of ROS by activated phagocytes via NADPH oxidase.

### S

**Secondary Immune Response**
Faster, stronger immune response upon antigen re-encounter, mediated by memory cells.

**Secretory IgA (sIgA)**
Dimeric IgA with secretory component, abundant in mucosal secretions.

**SMAC (Supramolecular Activation Cluster)**
Organized structure at the immunological synapse.

**Superantigen**
Antigen that binds outside the normal antigen-binding site, activating many T cells non-specifically. Causes toxic shock.

### T

**TGF-Î² (Transforming Growth Factor beta)**
Immunosuppressive cytokine that induces Tregs and promotes tissue repair.

**Th1, Th2, Th17**
Helper T cell subsets with distinct cytokine profiles and functions.

**Thymic Selection**
Process of positive and negative selection that shapes the T cell repertoire.

**TNF-Î± (Tumor Necrosis Factor alpha)**
Pro-inflammatory cytokine that activates endothelium, recruits neutrophils, and induces fever.

## ðŸ§¬ Expert Terms (Research/Clinical Level)

### A

**AIRE (Autoimmune Regulator)**
Transcription factor in medullary thymic epithelial cells that induces expression of tissue-specific antigens for negative selection.

**Allograft**
Transplanted tissue from a genetically non-identical donor of the same species.

**Antigen Processing**
Degradation of proteins into peptides for MHC presentation. Endogenous pathway (proteasome â†’ MHC-I) or exogenous pathway (endolysosomal â†’ MHC-II).

**APECED**
Autoimmune polyendocrinopathy-candidiasis-ectodermal dystrophy. Syndrome caused by AIRE mutations.

### B

**BLNK (B cell Linker protein)**
Adaptor protein in BCR signaling that nucleates signaling complexes.

**BTK (Bruton's Tyrosine Kinase)**
Tyrosine kinase in BCR signaling pathway. Mutations cause X-linked agammaglobulinemia.

### C

**cGAS-STING**
Cytosolic DNA sensor pathway. cGAS detects DNA, produces cGAMP, activates STING, leading to type I interferon production.

**Chronic Granulomatous Disease (CGD)**
Primary immunodeficiency due to NADPH oxidase defects, impairing ROS production.

**CLP (Common Lymphoid Progenitor)**
Hematopoietic progenitor committed to lymphoid lineage.

**CMP (Common Myeloid Progenitor)**
Hematopoietic progenitor committed to myeloid lineage.

### D

**DAMP (Damage-Associated Molecular Pattern)**
Endogenous molecules released from damaged cells that trigger inflammation (e.g., HMGB1, ATP, uric acid).

### E

**Exhaustion (T cell)**
State of T cell dysfunction after chronic antigen stimulation, characterized by high PD-1 expression and reduced effector function.

### F

**FoxP3**
Master transcription factor for regulatory T cells. Mutations cause IPEX syndrome.

### G

**Î³Î´ T cells**
T cells with gamma-delta TCRs (instead of alpha-beta). Enriched at epithelial barriers, recognize non-peptide antigens.

### H

**Hematopoietic Stem Cell (HSC)**
Self-renewing, multipotent stem cell that gives rise to all blood cells.

**HEV (High Endothelial Venule)**
Specialized blood vessels in lymph nodes that allow lymphocyte extravasation.

### I

**ILC (Innate Lymphoid Cell)**
Lymphoid cells without antigen receptors that produce cytokines. ILC1 (IFN-Î³), ILC2 (IL-5, IL-13), ILC3 (IL-17, IL-22).

**iNKT (invariant Natural Killer T) Cell**
T cells with invariant TCR that recognize lipid antigens presented by CD1d.

**IPEX**
Immune dysregulation, polyendocrinopathy, enteropathy, X-linked. Caused by FoxP3 mutations, lacking Tregs.

**ISG (Interferon-Stimulated Gene)**
Genes induced by interferon signaling, encoding antiviral effectors.

### J

**JAK-STAT Pathway**
Signaling pathway used by cytokine receptors. JAK kinases phosphorylate STATs, which translocate to nucleus.

### L

**LAT (Linker for Activation of T cells)**
Adaptor protein crucial for TCR signaling.

**LFA-1 (Lymphocyte Function-Associated antigen 1)**
Integrin (Î±LÎ²2) on leukocytes that mediates adhesion to endothelium and APCs.

### M

**MAIT (Mucosal-Associated Invariant T) Cell**
T cells with semi-invariant TCR that recognize bacterial riboflavin metabolites presented by MR1.

**Metabolic Reprogramming**
Shift in cellular metabolism (e.g., glycolysis vs. OXPHOS) that regulates immune cell differentiation and function.

**MR1**
MHC-I-related molecule that presents microbial metabolites to MAIT cells.

**MyD88**
Adaptor protein in TLR signaling that activates NF-ÎºB pathway.

### N

**NAPI (New Antigen Presenting Isoform)**
Rapid uptake mechanism in DCs for efficient antigen processing.

**NETosis**
Form of cell death where neutrophils release chromatin and antimicrobial proteins as extracellular traps (NETs).

**NLRP3**
Inflammasome sensor activated by diverse stimuli (ATP, crystals, pore-forming toxins).

### P

**PAD4 (Peptidyl Arginine Deiminase 4)**
Enzyme that citrullinates histones, enabling chromatin decondensation during NETosis.

**pDC (Plasmacytoid Dendritic Cell)**
DC subset specialized for massive type I interferon production in response to viruses.

**PD-1 (Programmed Death 1)**
Inhibitory receptor on T cells. Engagement induces T cell exhaustion. Target of cancer immunotherapy.

**Perforin**
Pore-forming protein released by CTLs and NK cells.

**Pyroptosis**
Inflammatory form of programmed cell death triggered by inflammasomes, involving gasdermin D pores.

### R

**RIG-I (Retinoic acid-Inducible Gene I)**
Cytoplasmic RNA sensor that detects viral RNA and triggers type I interferon production.

### S

**SCID (Severe Combined Immunodeficiency)**
Group of genetic disorders characterized by absence of T cells (and often B cells). "Bubble boy disease."

**Single-Cell RNA-seq (scRNA-seq)**
Technology that sequences transcriptomes of individual cells, revealing cellular heterogeneity.

**STAT (Signal Transducer and Activator of Transcription)**
Transcription factors activated by JAK kinases in cytokine signaling.

**Syk**
Tyrosine kinase recruited to phosphorylated ITAMs in BCR and other immune receptors.

### T

**TAM (Tumor-Associated Macrophage)**
Macrophages in tumor microenvironment, often M2-polarized and tumor-promoting.

**TdT (Terminal deoxynucleotidyl Transferase)**
Enzyme that adds N-nucleotides during V(D)J recombination.

**Tfh (T follicular helper) Cell**
CD4+ T cell subset that helps B cells in germinal centers.

**TIM-3**
Inhibitory receptor on exhausted T cells.

**Tissue-Resident Memory T cell (TRM)**
Non-circulating memory T cells permanently stationed in tissues.

**TRIF**
Adaptor protein in TLR3 and TLR4 signaling that activates IRF3 and type I interferon production.

### X

**Xenograft**
Transplanted tissue from a different species.

**XLA (X-Linked Agammaglobulinemia)**
B cell immunodeficiency caused by BTK mutations. No mature B cells or antibodies.

### Z

**ZAP-70 (Zeta-chain Associated Protein kinase 70)**
Tyrosine kinase crucial for TCR signaling. Mutations cause SCID.
